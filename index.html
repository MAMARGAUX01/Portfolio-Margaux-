UX Case Study Concept project Solo

Interior Design Project Management Platform

My RoleUX Researcher & Designer
ToolsFigma · Notion
TypeConcept / Portfolio
01Context

The landscape

Interior architecture projects involve many actors: designers, clients, contractors, suppliers and manufacturers. Projects can last several months and require constant coordination between all stakeholders.

During my work in an interior design studio, I observed that most project management still happens through a mix of phone calls, emails, spreadsheets and scattered documents. This creates delays, lost information and unnecessary stress for both designers and clients.

This project explores how a digital platform could centralise project communication, giving both the studio and the client a clearer view of the project at every stage.

02Problem Statement

The core tension

Interior designers need to coordinate complex projects involving many stakeholders, but the tools they currently use are fragmented and not adapted to their workflow.

Designers spend a large portion of their time chasing information instead of designing

Clients lack visibility on project progress and often feel uncertain during long projects

Information gets lost between emails, calls and documents

“The challenge: design a simple, transparent platform that improves designer–client communication while reducing studio administrative overhead.”

03Research & Discovery

Understanding the problem space

👁
Studio observation
Designers constantly switching between phone calls, emails and spreadsheets; clients requesting updates; supplier information hard to track.
🗣
Informal interviews
Conversations about daily coordination challenges, communication with clients, supplier tracking, and pain points in current workflows.
🔍
Competitive analysis
Asana, Monday, Notion, Houzz Pro, Studio Designer. Most are either too generic or not designed for direct client access.
04Key Insights

What the research revealed

01

Communication with clients consumes a large amount of time. Many interactions could be simplified with direct access to updates.

02

Information is scattered across multiple tools. Fragmentation increases risk of miscommunication and delays.

03

Clients want reassurance during long projects. Transparency plays a key role in building trust.

04

Designers want to focus on creativity. Any solution should reduce administrative overhead, not add to it.

05Solution

The proposed platform

Appointment system

Simple scheduling flow — clients book their first consultation without contacting the studio manually.

Project dashboard

Personalised project page with overview, moodboards, current phase, and milestones.

Project timeline

Visual timeline of project stages — improving transparency and reducing uncertainty for clients.

06Reflection & Learnings

What I learned

This project showed how deeply work practices influence product design. Interior designers care about how their tools reflect their professional image to clients.

“Next steps: testing with more designers, exploring mobile features for on-site updates, designing supplier management, and developing higher fidelity UI.”

Starting point

Working inside the studio gave me access to real friction — not a hypothetical version of it.

Design decision

I centred the platform on the client experience because client anxiety was the primary source of unnecessary designer workload.

Next step

Supplier management — linking deliveries to the client-facing timeline — is the most complex remaining problem.

Brand Strategy UX Research Product Concept Solo

YUHWA — K-Beauty Brand Concept

My RoleUX Research · Brand Strategy · Product Concept
Duration6 weeks
ToolsCanva · Adobe CC · Notion
TypeAcademic / Concept project
01Context

The landscape

K-beauty has become one of the most influential segments in the global skincare industry. Western consumers increasingly seek Korean skincare products for their innovation, layered routines and ingredient philosophy.

However, many brands entering Western markets face a challenge: balancing cultural authenticity with scientific credibility. This project explored how a new brand could occupy that space.

02Problem Statement

The core tension

“The challenge: design a brand concept that bridges Korean ingredient heritage with credible scientific validation — without losing either dimension.”

03Key Insights

What the research revealed

01

Western consumers seek authenticity. Traditional ingredients and rituals are part of the perceived value.

02

Scientific credibility builds trust. Clinical validation and dermatological research play a key role.

03

Brand identity shapes perceived product quality. A coherent brand universe is essential in the premium skincare market.

04Concept Development

YUHWA (요화)

YUHWA draws on the Korean word for softness and harmony. The concept centres on balance between traditional Korean herbal medicine (Hanbang) and modern dermatological science.

Product philosophy

Hanbang ingredients + dermatological research = cultural depth and scientific credibility in one.

Product architecture

A three-product ritual that simplifies the skincare routine while keeping the layered Korean skincare philosophy intact.

Visual identity

Muted natural palette, editorial typography, minimal packaging, bilingual brand language. Refined, calm, culturally grounded.

05Reflection & Learnings

What I learned

Branding is closely linked to user perception and trust. Consumers interpret signals from storytelling, design, and cultural references all at once.

The hardest challenge: translating Korean cultural elements without flattening or appropriating them for a Western audience.

The opportunity

The positioning map was the most clarifying tool — seeing where every major brand sat made the gap immediately visible.

Key tension

Balancing cultural authenticity with Western legibility, without flattening cultural nuance, was the hardest design challenge.

What I’d do next

Test the brand concept with both Korean and Western consumers to see whether cultural signals land as intended.

Botani'Kube — Smart Indoor Garden

My RoleMarket Research · Product Strategy
Team4 people
Duration6 weeks
ToolsGoogle Forms · Canva · Adobe CC · Notion
01Context

The landscape

Urban lifestyles increasingly limit access to outdoor space. Indoor gardening products have emerged as a way for city residents to reconnect with plants, even in small apartments.

Yet many consumers remain hesitant — not because of lack of interest, but because they’re afraid of failing.

02Problem Statement

The plant anxiety paradox

“A key reason people don’t buy plants is the fear of not being able to keep them alive. The challenge: how might a product remove that anxiety entirely?”

03Key Insights

What the research revealed

01

Plant care anxiety is a major barrier. Automation directly reduces this stress.

02

Consumers dislike restrictive ecosystems. Users prefer flexibility over proprietary capsule lock-in.

03

Sustainability matters. Reducing waste can be a strong differentiator for eco-conscious urban consumers.

04Solution

Botani'Kube

A smart indoor garden designed for urban apartments, integrating automated features to simplify plant care entirely.

Automated features

Automatic irrigation, integrated LED grow lighting, and smart humidity sensors — no daily maintenance needed.

Open seed ecosystem

Standard seeds rather than proprietary capsules — more freedom, less brand dependency.

Sustainability focus

Integrated compost module for reusing organic kitchen waste — supporting a more circular growing approach.

05Reflection & Learnings

What I learned

The most important insight was the role of problem framing. We initially approached this as a convenience product. Research revealed the real barrier was emotional anxiety — a fundamentally different problem to solve.

“This project reinforced the importance of deeply understanding user motivations before designing solutions.”

Problem framing

We almost designed around the wrong problem (convenience) until research revealed anxiety as the core blocker.

Key differentiator

The open seed standard was both functional and strategic — directly addressing competitor lock-in frustration.

What I’d do next

Validate whether anxiety framing resonates in user interviews, and test price point against willingness-to-pay.
